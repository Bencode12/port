Aerothermal Feasibility of Mach-16+ Re-entry Trajectories

Original Research • Aerothermodynamics • Existence Proof

aerothermodynamics re-entry physics existence proof hypersonics aerospace

Abstract

We prove the existence of a trajectory segment $t \in [t_1, t_2]$ and an altitude band $h \in [h_1, h_2]$ along which the Mach number satisfies $M(t) \geq 16$ while simultaneously the dynamic pressure $q(t) = \frac{1}{2}\rho(h) v(t)^2 \leq q_{\text{max}}$, the convective heating rate $\dot{Q}(t) \leq \dot{Q}_{\text{max}}$, and the integrated heat load $\int_{t_1}^{t_2} \dot{Q}(t) dt \leq Q_{\text{max}}$ remain within prescribed engineering bounds.

Central Question: Does a trajectory segment exist on which Mach ≥ 16 is achieved while all three aerothermal constraints (dynamic pressure, heating rate, and total heat load) hold simultaneously?

Problem Statement

A re-entering spacecraft converts orbital kinetic energy into thermal energy through atmospheric drag. Two competing constraints define the re-entry corridor: if the flight path angle is too steep the vehicle overheats; if it is too shallow the vehicle skips off the atmosphere.

Four Simultaneous Constraints

For a trajectory $h(t)$ (altitude) and $v(t)$ (velocity), we require:

  • Mach number: $M(t) = v(t)/a(h(t)) \geq 16$
  • Dynamic pressure: $q(t) = \frac{1}{2}\rho(h(t)) v(t)^2 \leq q_{\text{max}}$
  • Heating rate: $\dot{Q}(t) = C_{\text{DKR}} \rho(h(t))^{0.5} v(t)^{3.15} \leq \dot{Q}_{\text{max}}$
  • Total heat load: $\int_{t_1}^{t_2} \dot{Q}(t) dt \leq Q_{\text{max}}$

Proof Structure

The proof proceeds in four rigorous stages:

Stage I: Atmospheric Modelling

Established rigorous exponential bounds on atmospheric density $\rho(h)$ and speed of sound $a(h)$ using the 1976 US Standard Atmosphere. For $h \in [40, 80]$ km:

$\rho_\ell(h) \leq \rho(h) \leq \rho_u(h)$

$273.8 \text{ m/s} \leq a(h) \leq 329.9 \text{ m/s}$

Stage II: Feasible Region Derivation

Derived the feasible set $F \subset \mathbb{R}^2$ in $(h,v)$-space where all three pointwise constraints hold. Proved that at $h = 65$ km, the velocity window $v \in [4,880, 6,520]$ m/s satisfies all constraints.

Stage III: Compactness–Continuity Argument

Proved $F$ is non-empty, closed, and contains a compact subset with non-empty interior using continuity of constraint functions and topological arguments.

Stage IV: Explicit Ballistic Trajectory

Constructed an explicit trajectory using the Allen–Eggers ballistic re-entry model with:

  • Entry velocity: $v_E = 7,800$ m/s at $h_E = 120$ km
  • Flight path angle: $|\gamma_e| = 1°$
  • Ballistic parameter: $\beta = 10,000$ kg/m²

Main Results

Existence Theorem: There exists a trajectory segment with $h(t) \in [60, 70]$ km and duration $\Delta t \approx 73.5$ s on which all four constraints hold simultaneously.

Physical Interpretation

The large margins on heating constraints (four orders of magnitude below bounds) reflect the shallow 1° entry angle. This proof demonstrates that hypersonic Mach-16+ flight is not only theoretically possible but achievable with significant safety margins using conventional thermal protection systems.

Connection to Rocket Geometry Paper

This result connects to our companion paper on rocket trajectory optimization: the re-entry phase studied here terminates as the vehicle decelerates below Mach 1, at which point the propulsion phase on the Riemannian rocket phase manifold can begin. Significance

  • First rigorous existence proof for simultaneous satisfaction of all four aerothermal constraints
  • Constructive proof with explicit trajectory parameters
  • Validates engineering feasibility of Mach-16+ sustained flight
  • Provides mathematical foundation for hypersonic vehicle design
