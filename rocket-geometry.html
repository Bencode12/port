On the Differential Geometry of Ideal Rocket Trajectories

Original Research • Differential Geometry • Optimal Control

differential geometry Riemannian manifolds optimal control aerospace Hamiltonian mechanics

Abstract

A foundational differential-geometric treatment of ideal rocket trajectories under vacuum operation. We construct the rocket phase manifold $(M,g)$—a Riemannian 2-manifold on which the celebrated Tsiolkovsky delta-v relation emerges as a geodesic arc-length formula.

Key Result: The Tsiolkovsky equation $\Delta v = v_e \ln(m_0/m_f)$ is not merely an algebraic identity, but the arc-length of a geodesic on a flat Riemannian manifold.

Main Theorems

R1. Arc-Length Theorem

The Tsiolkovsky $\Delta v$ formula is the arc-length of a geodesic in the rocket phase manifold $(M,g)$. For any curve $\gamma(s) = (\varepsilon(s), v(s))$ with $\dot{v} \equiv 0$:

$L_g(\gamma) = v_e \ln\frac{1}{\varepsilon_1} = \Delta v$

R2. Geodesic Theorem

Constant-thrust burns are the unique geodesics of $(M,g)$. The geodesic equations yield:

$\varepsilon(t) = \varepsilon_0 e^{\alpha t}, \quad v(t) = v_0 - v_e \alpha t$

R3. Flatness Theorem

The Riemann curvature tensor vanishes identically: $R \equiv 0$. The manifold $(M,g)$ is globally flat and isometric to Euclidean $\mathbb{R}^2$ via the log-coordinate map.

R4. Conjugate-Point Theorem

No geodesic has conjugate points, implying every constant-thrust burn is globally optimal. This gives a rigorous geometric foundation for the primer-vector theorem.

R5. Lyapunov Stability Theorem

Reference geodesics are exponentially stable under bounded mass-flow perturbations, with the squared Riemannian distance serving as a natural Lyapunov function.

R6-R8. Advanced Results

  • Hamiltonian Equivalence: Geodesics are Hamiltonian trajectories on $(T^*M, \omega, H)$
  • Symplectic Reduction: For SO(2)-symmetric missions, the reduced space preserves structure
  • Staging Optimality: Multi-stage optimization via fiber-bundle splicing

Significance

This framework transforms rocket propulsion from algebraic formulas to intrinsic geometry. The flatness of $(M,g)$ explains why constant-thrust burns are globally optimal—a property that follows from the geometric structure rather than variational calculus alone.

Methods & Validation

The paper includes:

  • Complete proofs of all eight main theorems from first principles
  • Numerical validation across liquid-bipropellant, hybrid, and solid systems
  • Comparison with classical primer-vector theory
  • Extension to multi-stage vehicles with heterogeneous propellants
