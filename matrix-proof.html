Determinant Congruence for Sum-Entry Matrices

Original Proof • Linear Algebra

linear algebra matrix theory number theory proof techniques

Theorem Statement

Main Theorem: For any $n \times n$ matrix $A$ with entries $A_{ij} = i + j$, the determinant satisfies $\det(A) \equiv 0 \pmod{n}$ for all $n \geq 3$, and in fact $\det(A) = 0$ exactly.

Proof

Construction of the Matrix

Consider the $n \times n$ matrix $A$ defined by:

$A_{ij} = i + j \quad \text{for } 1 \leq i,j \leq n$

For example, when $n = 4$:

$A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 3 & 4 & 5 \\ 3 & 4 & 5 & 6 \\ 4 & 5 & 6 & 7 \\ 5 & 6 & 7 & 8 \end{pmatrix}$

Key Observation: Column Vectors

Let $c_j$ denote the $j$-th column of $A$. We can write each column as:

$c_j = \begin{pmatrix} 1+j \\ 2+j \\ 3+j \\ \vdots \\ n+j \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 3 \\ \vdots \\ n \end{pmatrix} + j \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \\ 1 \\ \vdots \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}$

Linear Dependence

Define two vectors:

$u = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \\ \vdots \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}, \quad v = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ \vdots \\ n \end{pmatrix}$

Every column $c_j$ can be expressed as a linear combination of $u$ and $v$:

$c_j = v + j \cdot u$

Since all $n$ columns lie in the span of just two vectors $\{u, v\}$, and $n \geq 3$, the columns are linearly dependent. Therefore, the column rank of $A$ is at most 2.

Conclusion

By the rank-nullity theorem, if $\text{rank}(A) \leq 2 < n$, then $A$ is singular, and hence:

$\det(A) = 0$

This holds for all $n \geq 3$. In particular, $\det(A) \equiv 0 \pmod{n}$ is trivially satisfied.

Edge Cases

Case $n = 1$

For $n = 1$: $A = (2)$, so $\det(A) = 2 \neq 0$.

Case $n = 2$

For $n = 2$:

$A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 3 \\ 3 & 4 \end{pmatrix}$

$\det(A) = 2 \cdot 4 - 3 \cdot 3 = 8 - 9 = -1 \neq 0$

Verification

The result can be verified computationally for small values of $n$:

  • $n = 3$: $\det(A) = 0$ ✓
  • $n = 4$: $\det(A) = 0$ ✓
  • $n = 5$: $\det(A) = 0$ ✓

Generalization

This proof technique extends to any matrix whose columns can be expressed as linear combinations of fewer than $n$ basis vectors. The key insight is recognizing the structural form $A_{ij} = f(i) + g(j)$, which immediately implies all columns lie in a 2-dimensional subspace.
